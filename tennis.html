Tennis

Number of players

  • Singles: Two players compete against each other, one representing each side of the court.
  • Doubles: Four players compete, with two on each side of the net forming teams.

    • Strategy of game

  • Hitting: Generate power and accuracy with various strokes (forehand, backhand, serve, etc.) to control the point and force the opponent into errors.
  • Positioning: Move effectively around the court to anticipate opponent's shots and reach them strategically.
  • Mindset: Maintain focus, composure, and adapt tactics based on the opponent's strengths and weaknesses.
  • Serving: Aim for power, placement, and variety to gain an advantage at the start of each point.

    • Is it a team game?

  • Primarily an individual sport in singles format, where players rely solely on their own skills and strategies.
  • Doubles involves teamwork and communication within the pair, coordinating movements and shot selection to outplay the opposing team.

    • Rules of the game

  • Each player/team gets one turn to hit the ball over the net and within the opponent's court before it bounces twice.
  • Points are scored when the opponent cannot return the ball legally.
  • Games are won by reaching a specific number of points with a two-point lead (except for sets decided by tie-breaks).
  • Matches typically consist of best-of-three or best-of-five sets.
  • Various other rules govern service faults, foot faults, line calls, and other situations.

    • Some important facts about the game

  • Ancient origins, traced back to 12th century Europe.
  • Modern tennis developed in England in the late 19th century.
  • Olympic sport since 1896.
  • Offers physical and mental challenges, requiring speed, agility, and strategic thinking.
  • Popular worldwide at recreational and professional levels.

    • Statistics/ Records

  • Fastest serve: Sam Groth (Australia) - 263 km/h (163 mph) (2015).
  • Most Grand Slam singles titles: Margaret Court (Australia) - 24 titles.
  • Most Wimbledon singles titles: Roger Federer (Switzerland) - 8 titles.
  • Longest match: John Isner (USA) vs Nicolas Mahut (France) - 11 hours, 5 minutes (2010 Wimbledon).
